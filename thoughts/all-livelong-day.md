# The Livelong Day

"What a bloody long day"

Days where the passing of time feels full and whole and every minute feels like a lifetime. Time doesn’t pass so much as just accumulate with a slow drag. You want nothing more than for it to move, but the wanting only seems to slow it further.

According to Collins and his [dictionary](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/livelong) :

> adjective mainly poetic
> 1. (of time) long or  [seemingly](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/seemingly "Definition of seemingly")  long, esp in a  [tedious](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/tedious "Definition of tedious")  way (esp in the phrase  all the livelong  [day](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/day "Definition of day"))
> 2. [whole](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/whole "Definition of whole");  [entire](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/entire "Definition of entire")

We take this word as this monotonously long feeling that a day feels much longer than the reality in time.

The feeling here has been explored for centuries across sound, poetry and the visual arts. There is a companion thought [here]() that explores some of these.

## The Original Meaning

The phrase ‘livelong day’ has shifted meaning considerably and one can wonder if this shift tells us something.

The word has Germanic roots, with traces in Old English as far back as the 15th century. At its origin, it had nothing to do with endurance. It meant something closer to the opposite.  The [etymology timeline](https://www.etymonline.com/word/livelong):

**Old English** (adjective) 1250CE
>*lief/loef* meaning dear, valued, beloved, pleasant

**Old English** (noun) ~ 450CE - 1250CE
> *leof* meaning a beloved person, friend

**Middle English**
>*leve-long, lefe-long*  meaning esteemed, beloved, dear

**Contemporary English** idiom
> all the livelong day meaning a long, especially tedious, day


Another example of this use can be seen in the Odyssey of Homer (~700BCE), translated by Samuel Butler in 1900. It could be argued that the same beloved interpretation of the word was still present during the time of the translation.
> **Book IX** - '[..]through the **livelong** day to the going down of the sun we ate and drank our fill,- and we had plenty of wine left, for each one of us had taken many jars full when we sacked the city of the Cicons, and this had not yet run out. While we were feasting we kept turning our eyes towards the land of the Cyclopes, which was hard by, and saw the smoke of their stubble fires.
>
> **Book XII** - '[..] and feasted through the **livelong** day to the going down of the sun, but when the sun had set and it came on dark, the men laid themselves down to sleep by the stern cables of the ship.'


**Old German idiom**
>*den lieben langen Tag* meaning the dear long day (often used to describe children playing all day)

**Contemporary German idiom**
> *den lieben langen Tag* (still in use)




## From a beloved day to an awfully long one

It is seen that the meaning shifted during the early 20th century, around the same time as the [Second Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Industrial_Revolution).

A word that once showed appreciation for the beauty in the passing of time then came to represent the excruciation of it.

The *livelong day* became the *working day*. Full of repetitive labour & exhaustion.

I find myself wondering whether this is simply a linguistic curiosity, or whether it's something more like a record, perhaps a trace of how people’s relationship with time changed.

The industrial clock didn't just structure the day; it seems to have recoloured the language around it.

And so, in a time where the day was not treasured but took perseverance to finish, worker's music emerged.

## In Worker's Music

Worker's music has been around for as long as people have been working. The idea of using rhythm to carve time into bearable pieces, to make hours that no longer belonged to them feel, at least in part, survivable, is something that has been around for hundreds of years and bares roots in many genres of music, blues, folk, country, rock, punk etc.

Such music was not just expressions of labour, but perhaps technologies of endurance.

One song comes to mind here: *I’ve been working on the railroad all the livelong day*. This song has seen many changes over time. There are some online sources [[1]](https://balladofamerica.org/ive-been-working-on-the-railroad/) [[2]](https://slavery.princeton.edu/stories/the-minstrel-tradition-at-princeton-university) that suggest the song, originally titled *Levee Song*, was first sung by African American slaves working on a Mississippi River levee. An injustice to be noted here in the usage of the song by students performing blackface at [Princeton University](https://slavery.princeton.edu/stories/the-minstrel-tradition-at-princeton-university) in as late as 1949.

African American folk song (1830s), Carmina Princetonia (1894), Pete Seeger (1964), Johnny Cash (1973), John Denver (1997).

The phrase embedded itself further into the hard-working culture and further from its heart-felt origin.

## What we’ve lost

To summarise this thought, English once had a word for the dear, long, beloved day. A day that felt full not because it was slow, but because it was loved.

The clock became a tool of production rather than a marker of the sun's arc and so our words bent accordingly.

But what's harder to account for is the loss of the original meaning, the idea that a long day could be a gift rather than a sentence. Not because life was easier, or slower, or less brutal. It definitely wasn't. But somewhere in the language was the possibility of holding both things at once: the length and the love of it.

Whether that possibility still exists, somewhere beneath the idiom, is not a question language alone can answer.
