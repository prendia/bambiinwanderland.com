# Painting Restoration

## Intro

There is a fine art painting restorer and conservationist based in Chicago by the name Julian Baumgartner. They have a YouTube channel, [Baumgartner Restoration](https://youtube.com/@baumgartnerrestoration?si=pg-VJfRYZSK8tteI), in which they use specialist techniques to almost heal old, damaged artwork and provide protection for the art to survive into future generations.

I find many of the videos very soothing. There's something about the restoration process that makes me reflect on my own life in many ways.

In many of the old paintings Baumgartner receives, there are huge sections with paint completely missing. He must make an educated guess on what the artists intended, based on the rest of the image. Making decisions about what was there, based on what you know of the artists, is arguably more intimate than just preserving what remains. It's almost as if you're in an active conversation with the artist, and more importantly, what the artists was trying to say.

## Holding space for someone you'll never meet

I can imagine receiving a painting from an unknown artists can be a very layered experience.

A good restorer attempts to restore the same colour, line work, and texture that the original artists used. In Baumgartner's videos, actually, it is very common for pieces to be smeared with the previous restorer's idea of what the painting should look like. These are often very painful to witness.

So why does this happen - how do some people get it so wrong?

## Decision-making in the restoration process

I do wonder how painting restorers such as Baumgartner navigate the decision-making process, especially in situations where much of the painting is missing. So many questions.

Every decision carries weight. Do you restore what was likely there, or what feels right to you? Do you follow the logic of the composition, or trust your gut? And perhaps most pressingly, how do you know when to stop? I can see there may be a real difficulty in over-correcting, in letting your own hand linger too long on someone else's work until it quietly becomes your own. The best restorers seem to know that restraint is its own kind of skill.

To do just enough, and no more.

Perhaps you have information on who the artist was, where they came from, what they stood for and what they didn't. Perhaps you agree with everything they were trying to say.

But what if you didn't agree? What if they had wildly differing views to your own? What if they had no views at all?

Is the act of preserving what it was this unknown person was trying to say equally as important whether or not you agree with their artistic decisions? Or perhaps their life decisions?

The act of restoration itself is an act of holding space for an artist's intention even when you might not know or agree with who they really were.

And one final question.

> What does this really feel like~ to subordinate your own taste in service of someone else's vision?
